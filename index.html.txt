    <!-- TIMELINE -->
    <section id="timeline" class="timeline" aria-labelledby="timeline-heading">
      <h2 id="timeline-heading">Journey Through Time</h2>
      <p>Key events and cultural shifts that shaped Chinese New Year.</p>

      <!-- Event 1 -->
      <article class="event" aria-labelledby="e1">
        <h4 id="e1">1. Myth of the Nian (Origins)</h4>
        <p class="meta">Origins — Traditional myth</p>
        <p>Legend tells of the Nian, a monster frightened by loud noises, red paper, and bright lights — origins of firecrackers, red decorations, and lanterns.</p>
      </article>

      <!-- Event 2 -->
      <article class="event" aria-labelledby="e2">
        <h4 id="e2">2. Early Dynastic Rituals</h4>
        <p class="meta">Zhou & early imperial period</p>
        <p>Spring festivals included agricultural rites and family offerings — the emphasis on renewal and ancestral respect grows.</p>
      </article>

      <!-- Event 3 -->
      <article class="event" aria-labelledby="e3">
        <h4 id="e3">3. Tang Dynasty — Festivals Flourish</h4>
        <p class="meta">Tang dynasty (618–907)</p>
        <p>Large public celebrations and lantern festivals become popular cultural features during the Tang era.</p>
      </article>

      <!-- Event 4 -->
      <article class="event" aria-labelledby="e4">
        <h4 id="e4">4. Song Dynasty — Lanterns & Urban Culture</h4>
        <p class="meta">Song dynasty (960–1279)</p>
        <p>Urbanization brings new festival culture: markets, lantern shows, and family reunions in growing towns and cities.</p>
      </article>

      <!-- Event 5 -->
      <article class="event" aria-labelledby="e5">
        <h4 id="e5">5. Ming & Qing — Performances and Processions</h4>
        <p class="meta">Ming (1368–1644) & Qing (1644–1912)</p>
        <p>Dragon and lion dances, temple fairs, and theatrical performances spread widely as forms of communal celebration.</p>
      </article>

      <!-- Event 6 -->
      <article class="event" aria-labelledby="e6">
        <h4 id="e6">6. Regional Variations Grow</h4>
        <p class="meta">Local customs develop</p>
        <p>Different provinces and later overseas communities adapt rituals: food, dress, and local ceremonies differ by region.</p>
      </article>

      <!-- Event 7 -->
      <article class="event" aria-labelledby="e7">
        <h4 id="e7">7. Republican Era — Media & Modernity</h4>
        <p class="meta">20th century changes</p>
        <p>Print media and later radio/television begin shaping a shared national culture and popularizing certain festival elements.</p>
      </article>

      <!-- Event 8 -->
      <article class="event" aria-labelledby="e8">
        <h4 id="e8">8. PRC Era — Official Holidays & Revival</h4>
        <p class="meta">People's Republic of China (1949–)</p>
        <p>After periods of restriction, the Spring Festival regains prominence and becomes an important public holiday and travel season.</p>
      </article>

      <!-- Event 9 -->
      <article class="event" aria-labelledby="e9">
        <h4 id="e9">9. Digital Age — Red Packets Go Online</h4>
        <p class="meta">21st century</p>
        <p>Digital red envelopes, livestream galas, and social media reshape how people celebrate — more sharing online, less physical exchange in some contexts.</p>
      </article>

      <!-- Event 10 -->
      <article class="event" aria-labelledby="e10">
        <h4 id="e10">10. Global Diaspora & Fusion</h4>
        <p class="meta">Modern global celebrations</p>
        <p>Communities worldwide celebrate with parades, multicultural fusion events, and local adaptations — making CNY a global cultural phenomenon.</p>
      </article>

    </section>
